Managing GLP-1 Side Effects

Updated Feb 2026 ¬∑ 10 min read

Most GLP-1 side effects are manageable and improve over time. Here's what to expect and how to minimize discomfort during your treatment.

Common Side Effects

Gastrointestinal symptoms are the most common side effects of GLP-1 medications. They typically occur during dose escalation and improve as your body adjusts.

Nausea

Very common (30-50% of patients)

The most frequently reported side effect. Usually mild to moderate and improves within weeks.

  • Eat smaller, more frequent meals
  • Avoid high-fat and greasy foods
  • Eat slowly and stop when you first feel full
  • Stay hydrated with small sips throughout the day
  • Ginger (tea, chews, or supplements) can help

Vomiting

Common (10-20% of patients)

Often related to eating too much or too quickly. More common during dose increases.

  • Same strategies as nausea management
  • Don't lie down immediately after eating
  • Consider staying at current dose longer before increasing

Diarrhea

Common (15-25% of patients)

May occur as your digestive system adjusts to the medication.

  • Stay well hydrated
  • Avoid artificial sweeteners
  • Reduce fatty foods temporarily
  • Consider probiotics

Constipation

Common (15-25% of patients)

Slowed digestion can lead to constipation. Reduced food intake also means less bulk.

  • Increase fiber intake (vegetables, fruits, whole grains)
  • Drink plenty of water
  • Stay physically active
  • Consider a fiber supplement if needed

Injection Site Reactions

Occasional (5-10% of patients)

Redness, itching, or mild pain at the injection site.

  • Rotate injection sites (abdomen, thigh, upper arm)
  • Let medication reach room temperature before injecting
  • Apply a cool compress if needed

Less Common Side Effects

Fatigue

Some patients report tiredness, especially in the first few weeks. This often relates to reduced calorie intake. Ensure you're eating enough protein and getting adequate nutrition despite reduced appetite.

Headache

Usually temporary and mild. Stay hydrated and ensure you're not skipping meals entirely.

Dizziness

Can occur with blood sugar changes or dehydration. Monitor if you have diabetes.

Hair Loss

Rapid weight loss from any cause can trigger temporary hair shedding (telogen effluvium). This is related to the weight loss itself, not the medication directly. It typically resolves as weight stabilizes.

Pancreatitis

Inflammation of the pancreas is a rare but serious risk. Symptoms include severe abdominal pain that may radiate to your back, nausea, and vomiting. Stop the medication and seek medical care immediately if suspected.

Gallbladder Disease

Rapid weight loss increases gallstone risk with any method. GLP-1 medications may slightly increase this risk. Symptoms include pain in the upper right abdomen, especially after eating fatty foods.

Thyroid Tumors

GLP-1 medications carry a boxed warning about thyroid C-cell tumors seen in rodent studies. This hasn't been confirmed in humans, but these medications are contraindicated if you have a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2).

Managing Side Effects: General Strategies

During Dose Titration

  • Go slow: Don't rush to increase your dose. Stay at each level until side effects are manageable.
  • Communicate: Tell your doctor about persistent symptoms. They may adjust your titration schedule.
  • Be patient: Most side effects improve significantly over 4-8 weeks.

Dietary Adjustments

  • Eat smaller portions more frequently
  • Prioritize protein at each meal
  • Reduce fatty, greasy, and fried foods
  • Avoid eating until you're overly full
  • Don't drink large amounts of liquid with meals

Lifestyle Factors

  • Stay physically active (helps with constipation and overall well-being)
  • Get adequate sleep
  • Manage stress (can worsen GI symptoms)
  • Consider working with a registered dietitian

When Side Effects Don't Improve

If side effects remain problematic despite these strategies:

  • Your doctor may recommend a slower titration schedule
  • You might try a different GLP-1 medication (some people tolerate one better than another)
  • Anti-nausea medications can be prescribed for temporary relief
  • In rare cases, the medication may not be right for you

Most patients find that side effects are manageable and worth the benefits. The key is communication with your healthcare team and patience during the adjustment period.
